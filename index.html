A Submission Toward Treasury Reform

A working foundation
for cross-border payments.

Two targeted reforms. The first brings foreign stablecoin issuers within Australia's regulatory perimeter and opens a world-first cross-border testing corridor. The second gives the Reserve Bank the legal infrastructure to issue a wholesale CBDC if a public interest case emerges. Use the simulators below to model each.

Reform 01 · Stablecoins · Extraterritorial reach + testing corridor Reform 02 · CBDC · Enabling Act
Reform 01 · Stablecoins
Extend the perimeter.
Open the corridor.

Australia's Tranche 1 framework applies only to constitutionally covered corporations. Foreign stablecoin issuers, including USDC and USDT, which already settle the majority of AUD-denominated stablecoin volume, sit outside its scope.

The EU resolved this through MiCA's extraterritorial reach in 2024. The UK opened a dedicated stablecoin cohort within the FCA Sandbox the same year. No jurisdiction yet operates a dedicated cross-border stablecoin testing corridor. Australia could be the first.

Reform 02 · CBDC
Optionality,
not compulsion.

The RBA has signalled a clear position: regulate the private stablecoin market now; build the legal foundation for a wholesale CBDC so that if the public interest case strengthens, the legislative groundwork is ready.

A CBDC Enabling Act does not compel issuance. It defines what a CBDC is, authorises the RBA to issue one if a public interest case emerges, and establishes a governance framework. Nothing more.

This simulator models the response readiness of the Australian state across two scenarios, with and without the Act, when public interest pressure arrives.
